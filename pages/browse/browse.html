The Grotto Trip Gallery
Dog Hill-Donnehue Cave System
After changing into our caving gear, Bloomington Indiana Grotto memebers gather at the entrance to Dog Hill Cave. Everyone needs knee pads, a helmet, headlamp, and a waterproof backpack to store webbing and extra lights/batteries. The landowners are very kind to let us explore their private cave!
One of my favorite caving activities is exploring side passages that are very small. Here two of my cave buddies are seen crawling through a passage that leads to nowhere. However, we still enjoy the physical challenge and end up finding interesting fossils.
There are many large rimstone dams in this cave system. Here I climb up one of the larger ones. The neat thing about rimstone is that the surface of the formation has a lot of grip. This is very helpful when climbing and prevents slipping.
My favorite features of a cave are those that are created by flowing water. Here the side walls are very smooth, yet jagged on the edges. We are deeper in the cave now and the water is becoming very clear and beautiful!
This is one of the largest rooms in Dog Hill and it also contains some of the most impressive formations in the whole cave system. This is a good place to stop for pictures and possibly a little snack. We stopped to wait for the rest of the group to catch up.
Here is a very beautiful formation that has formed over the entrance to the next passage. To gain access to the next room we needed to crawl under the formation and though the water. It was not a very hard crawl but it was fun!
This is an example of an extremely tight crawling passage that goes over the top of the bathtub. Only very small cavers can fit through the passage. Larger cavers must access the next room via the bathtub.
This is one fo the "bathtubs" found in Dog Hill. A "bathtub" refers to a small flooded crawling passage with only a few inches of breathing room. Ropes are installed to help guide you through the passage since it is very difficult to see the end of a bathtub.
Here my cave buddy Mark completes his first "Bathtub". We take turn going one by one so that we do not create wakes in the passage. Creating wakes minimizes airspace even more. We all end up with wet ears due to what cavers refer to as "ear dips" or turning your head to the side to breath and submerging half of your head.
This is the rope climb that leads to the Dog Hill-Donnehue connection point. We did not attempt the climb this trip as the group was too big and inexperienced. My next trip I will enter from Donnehue and decend into Dog Hill via rigging. But for now we return the way we came in!
